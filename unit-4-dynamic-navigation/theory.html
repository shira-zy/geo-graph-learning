Section divider for theory beat #3 (~8-10 min) — the road-routing ladder. This is the densest beat; we pace it with the background-bridge sidebar first for students without classical-algorithms exposure.

The background-bridge sidebar, explicitly for the cohort without classical- algorithms exposure (a known risk in this unit). Keep the on-slide content to the one figure + one line; the depth is here in the note. Mini-lecture: Dijkstra grows a frontier outward from the origin, always settling the closest-so-far node and "relaxing" (improving) its neighbours' tentative costs; because weights are non- negative, once a node is settled its cost is final. A* adds a heuristic h(n) — an estimate of remaining cost — so the frontier grows toward the goal instead of in all directions; if h never overestimates (admissible) the answer is still optimal. TDSP is A*/Dijkstra where the edge cost is read from w(edge, clock) at the moment you arrive at the edge; under FIFO it stays correct. Reassure students: you don't have to implement these — you have to DIRECT an AI to, and name them correctly.

Beat #3, NEW (Ben's item 1), sits between the background bridge and the admissible- heuristic slide. The pseudocode IS the visual — a code slide. Read the two panels side by side: they share the SAME skeleton — initialise dist (g) to infinity except the source at 0; seed a min-priority queue; then loop: pop the minimum-key node, SETTLE it (its cost is now final — safe because weights are non-negative), and RELAX each outgoing edge (if going through u beats v's current best, lower g[v], set parent[v]=u, and re-key v in the queue). The ONLY difference is the highlighted priority key: Dijkstra keys the queue by g[u] (cost-so-far), A* by f[u]=g[u]+h[u] (cost-so-far PLUS the heuristic hint toward the goal), and the push key changes to match. Say the load-bearing line aloud: "A* with h≡0 is EXACTLY Dijkstra" — h=0 makes f=g and the two panels collapse into one. This is why the next slides can treat admissibility as the only thing that matters: the search machinery is identical. Cite Dijkstra 1959 / Hart-Nilsson-Raphael 1968. Anticipated question: "why does settling stay correct?" — non-negative edge weights mean once you pop the minimum, no cheaper route to it can exist. Demo tie: Beat 4 runs NetworkX's A*.

Beat #3 / demo Beat 4 (the "squeeze valve" / trimmable beat). Read the formula: g(n) is the known cost from the start to n; h(n) is the ESTIMATED remaining cost to the goal; A* always expands the open node with the smallest f = g + h, so the search leans toward the goal. h*(n) is the TRUE remaining cost — admissibility, h(n) <= h*(n), is exactly "never over-promise," and it is what preserves optimality. The figure makes admissibility visual: Dijkstra explores a disk in all directions (~109 nodes); A* explores an ellipse hugging the A-B line (~78). Same optimal path. The load-bearing term is "admissible" — define it before leaning on it: an over-estimating heuristic can return a WRONG (sub-optimal) path, which is exactly the trade-off the frontier beat (#7) revisits with learned heuristics. Anticipated question: "why is great-circle/max-speed admissible?" — because the true remaining travel time can never be less than the straight-line distance flown at the fastest legal speed; it is a guaranteed lower bound. Demo: Beat 4 runs A* and compares node counts.

Beat #3 — the heuristic menu (NEW). The previous slide taught only great-circle; this one lays out the spectrum so a student can DIRECT an AI past the default. Read the figure left to right as an informedness axis: h=0 is Dijkstra (no hint, explores everywhere); great-circle/max-speed is the simple admissible default; LANDMARK/ALT precomputes shortest-path distances to a handful of landmarks and uses the triangle inequality to get a TIGHTER lower bound that is still admissible — so it explores fewer nodes for free at query time; WEIGHTED A* inflates the heuristic (f = g + eps*h, eps>1) to push harder toward the goal — faster, but now only eps-suboptimal (you may not get the true shortest path); LEARNED h (a model predicts h) sits furthest right — potentially very tight but with NO admissibility guarantee. The production answer for road networks is usually PREPROCESSING — contraction hierarchies — which buys microsecond queries at the cost of a big one-time build. The single load-bearing idea: as you move right you explore fewer nodes, but you pay in preprocessing and you risk losing the optimality guarantee. This sets up Beat 7's learned-heuristic frontier as just the far-right end of this same axis. No demo cell computes ALT/weighted A* — they are taught-not-computed here; the demo runs plain admissible A* (Beat 4). Anticipated question: "which should I pick?" — great-circle unless it's too slow; then ALT keeps optimality, weighted A*/learned trade it away.

Beat #3, NEW (Ben's items 2+3), right after the heuristic menu. Makes "admissible" concrete with a worked comparison on ONE start→goal. Read the figure's four panels: (i) h≡0 is Dijkstra — it fans out in a disk and explores the MOST nodes (~121); (ii) great-circle/v_max is admissible — an elongated set hugging the S–G line, FEWER nodes (~78); (iii) a TIGHTER admissible bound (landmark/ALT) — FEWEST (~41); and crucially all three return the IDENTICAL optimal path (the dashed line). Panel (iv) is the warning: an INADMISSIBLE h that overestimates explores few nodes but returns the red, sub-optimal path — the optimality guarantee is gone. The single lesson: valid = admissible = never overestimate; a tighter admissible h explores fewer nodes for the SAME answer; inadmissible buys speed by breaking correctness. Then the transit case (bullet 3): on a time-dependent / time-expanded transit graph, the admissible TIME lower bound is straight-line distance to the target divided by the network's fastest vehicle speed — you can never beat flying straight at top speed — and h≡0 again recovers Dijkstra. Bullet 4 is the punchline for the next section: RAPTOR and CSA are attractive precisely because they are schedule-native and need NO heuristic at all — there's no graph to guide a search over. This is a taught-not-computed slide; the demo's Beat 4 runs only plain admissible A*. Beat #4 / demo Beat 7b — the fix. Say the name in full first: RAPTOR = Round-bAsed Public Transit Optimized Router (Delling, Pajor & Werneck 2015) — the "round-based" is the whole idea, so the acronym itself previews the mechanism. The invariant as a formula: tau_k[p] is RAPTOR's label array — the earliest known arrival time at stop p reachable with AT MOST k trips (k-1 transfers). Each round k extends the previous round's labels by scanning every route once, so tau is monotone non-increasing in k and the whole Pareto frontier (arrival vs transfers) is just the sequence of tau arrays. RAPTOR is first-class: this is the elevated transit-routing content the outline insists on. The figure: round 0 seeds the origin; round 1 = best with one ride (0 transfers); round 2 allows one transfer; it plateaus. Stress the invariant ("rounds = transfers") — it's the prompt-grade mental model. Because it needs no preprocessing, RAPTOR works in fully dynamic (delay) scenarios — a real advantage over preprocessed methods. It's order-of- magnitude faster than graph-based approaches on metro networks. Demo Beat 7b steps through a ~110-line vendored RAPTOR and prints the real per-round arrivals. Anticipated question: "where do the labels on the figure come from?" — the demo fills them with the notebook's actual per-round arrivals once raptor() runs.

Beat #4 — RAPTOR internals (NEW, split off the previous slide so neither crams). This is Ben's point (6): make explicit that RAPTOR is not magic — its input is PRE-BUILT from GTFS. The demo's build_raptor_model() does exactly three things: (1) group trips that share an ordered stop-sequence into a "route" (pattern), sorted by departure; (2) keep each trip's per-stop timetable (TRIP_STOPS); (3) build the STOP_ROUTES index = for each stop, which routes pass through it and at what position. With that, each round k is two phases: phase (i) collects every route touching a stop improved last round and SCANS it forward once, riding the earliest catchable trip and relaxing downstream stops (this is the route-scan loop in the demo's raptor()); phase (ii) takes the stops improved this round and RELAXES FOOTPATH TRANSFERS — walk to nearby stops via FOOT_ADJ — which may set up next round's boardings. The teaching emphasis: the walking/transfer step is a FIRST-CLASS phase of every round, not an afterthought, and it is the seam where the transit and walking networks couple — which the next slide unpacks. Demo: cell Beat-7b cells build the model and step the rounds. Anticipated question: "why group trips into routes at all?" — so a single linear sweep relaxes a whole line's downstream stops at once; that grouping is what makes RAPTOR fast.

NEW (Ben's item B) — the RAPTOR round loop as pseudocode, between "Building RAPTOR" (the model) and the multimodal-network anatomy. The pseudocode IS the visual; read it line by line, grounding each line in the demo's vendored raptor(): - Line 1: τ[·]=∞, τ[src]=t₀, mark the source — the demo's arrival=defaultdict(INF), arrival[origin_stop]=dep_s, marked={origin_stop}. - "for k=1,2,…": round k returns journeys with ≤ k trips (k−1 transfers) — the demo's for k in range(1, max_rounds+1). Rounds ARE transfers. - PHASE 1 (ride): the demo collects every route touching a marked stop (STOP_ROUTES), then scans each route ONCE forward from its earliest improved stop, boarding the earliest catchable trip (_earliest_trip) and relaxing every downstream stop: if the trip's arrival beats τ[p], lower τ[p] and mark p. Grouping trips into routes (build_raptor_model) is exactly WHY this is a single forward sweep per line — no priority queue, no graph. - PHASE 2 (walk): take the stops marked in Phase 1 and relax FOOTPATH transfers — the demo's FOOT_ADJ, a precomputed table of nearby-stop pairs (KD-tree over projected coords, WALK_M = 250 m) with a walk time (distance / WALK_MPS, 1.2 m/s) that serves as the minimum transfer time. If τ[p]+w beats τ[p′], lower τ[p′] and mark p′. This is the ONLY "walk" RAPTOR knows, and it runs in EVERY round — that alternation is how walking is woven in, not bolted on. - Termination: if no stop was marked this round, stop — the demo's "if not marked: break". The marked set carried from Phase 2 into the next round's Phase 1 is the seam: a transfer walk found now sets up next round's boardings. Anti-loop: footpaths are pruned/short (Coupling slide) so you never board → walk a few metres → re-board the same line. Demo tie: cells build build_raptor_model + FOOT_ADJ, then step raptor() and print per-round arrivals. Anticipated question: "does freezing t₀ lose exactness?" — no; RAPTOR reads each trip's actual scheduled arrival, so within the timetable it is exact. Beat #4 — the multimodal coupling slide (NEW). Serves Ben's points (4), (6), and (1) at once: HOW the modes actually join. Beat #4, NEW BONUS (Ben's item 5) — a mid-deck optional slide (marked skippable in the title), placed right after Coupling because it is the SAME footpath-discipline lesson at station scale. The point: a big terminal (a metro interchange, a central bus station) is NOT a single GTFS stop — it is a cluster of PLATFORM stops, each its own stop_id (each carries its own arriving trips), grouped under one parent_station. Moving between platforms is not free: it costs a walk PLUS a minimum change time (climb stairs, cross a concourse). GTFS models this with parent_station on stops.txt plus pathways.txt / transfers.txt carrying min_transfer_time. Two modeling options (same as the footpath choice on the Coupling slide): collapse the station to a single SUPER-NODE, or keep platform stops as distinct nodes joined by intra-station footpath edges that carry the min change time (the figure shows the latter). Why it matters — the honest failure modes: too permissive and you allow IMPOSSIBLE instant cross-platform transfers (a journey that can't physically happen); too strict and you FORBID valid connections a rider would actually make. It's the min-transfer-time and footpath-pruning discipline from the previous slide, now inside one station. Flag it as advanced/optional: the demo's clipped feed treats stops flatly, so this is a "here's what production needs" pointer, not a demo cell. Point students at GTFS pathways.txt. Anticipated question: "does our demo do this?" — no; it's why our straight-line 250 m footpaths are a simplification, and the upgrade path is real station modeling.

Our "Cost of Anarchy" deliberately does NOT build a congestion model where edge delay feeds back on flow, so it is not an equilibrium quantity. Beat #7 part 1 (concept). Surface the speaker-note clarity onto the slide (Ben's point 7): (a) WHAT a learned heuristic is — a model (PHIL uses a GNN) that PREDICTS the remaining-cost estimate h(n) at a node, in place of the hand-built great-circle / max-speed formula from the A* slide; (b) WHY it explores fewer nodes — a better- informed h makes A*'s f = g + h rank goal-ward nodes first, so the frontier leans hard toward the goal instead of fanning out (the left panel of the figure shows the goal-biased frontier); (c) the COST — a predicted h carries NO admissibility guarantee, so the returned path may be sub-optimal, which means you must VERIFY (re-check optimality or bound the error).
