Define the load-bearing frame words before leaning on them. LAGRANGIAN = you ride WITH the particle (the bus) and record where it is over time — that's GPS. EULERIAN = you stand at a fixed gauge and count what passes — that's a loop detector / sensor (Unit 3). The figure contrasts them literally: left, sampled positions of one moving bus; right, three fixed sensors tallying vehicles. Why it matters: the two frames make different things easy — Lagrangian gives you a route shape but sparse coverage; Eulerian gives you dense flow at a point but no path. Tell the room U3 picks up exactly where this leaves off. Vocabulary plant: trajectory, sampling rate / temporal resolution.

The formal definition slide — plant the exact object everything downstream (matching, inference, DTW) operates on, so later slides can lean on it. Read both forms off the figure: LEFT, the spatio-temporal trajectory is timestamped points in order; because each carries a time, you can DERIVE speed and bearing from Δposition/Δt (the red arrow) — that derived bearing is exactly what the Step-8 direction-aware matcher uses. RIGHT, drop the timestamps and you keep an ordered POLYLINE: shape and order, no timing — that's the route form a geometric metric (DTW/Fréchet) compares. The single most consequential property is the SAMPLING RATE: dense (seconds, e.g. a car logger) vs sparse (minutes, our buses at ~1 fix/min). Sparse is WHY the between-ping gap dominates (next beat) and why plain Kalman fails — name it here so the whole unit has a home for "sparse." Anticipated question: "is order really part of the definition?" — yes, ordering is intrinsic; an unordered point cloud is NOT a trajectory (foreshadows the directed-bearing slide in Beat 4).

Reframe "cleanup" as "modeling". The left panel is jitter — scatter of a few metres around the true road from multipath + device precision; that's the kind of noise a smoother handles. The right panel is the REAL problem in our regime: with a fix only once a minute, the bus travels hundreds of metres unobserved, and several distinct road paths are all consistent with the two endpoints — the "?" in the figure. The load-bearing claim of the beat, given its own slide so it lands. Students (and naive agents) reach for "just get more data." Refuse it: more pings = denser cloud, but density is not disambiguation — a thousand pings between two intersections still don't tell you which of three parallel streets the bus took, because that information was never sampled. What resolves it is a MODEL: a prior on observation noise (emission) and a prior on plausible motion (the road network as transition). That's the exact pair the HMM supplies (Beat 4) and the same pair map inference reuses backward (Beat 5). No figure here — it's a one-claim slide; the depth is the talk track.

Give the mechanism honestly before the critique. A Kalman filter keeps a running estimate of the bus's state (position + velocity), predicts the next state from a motion model, then corrects it with each noisy measurement; Q says how much you trust the motion model, R how much you trust the GPS. At HIGH sampling rates it's the right tool and it recurs in U3. The figure foreshadows the failure (next slide): the smoothed teal line is prettier than the raw pings but it cuts across the L-bend — it has no idea a road is there. filterpy is the demo's choice precisely because its explicit F/H/Q/R is transparent. Vocabulary: state-space / motion model, process vs measurement noise.

(If short on time, summarize verbally — the figure + the K-intuition line carry it.) This is the heart of the theory, so give it air. Read the state vector first: we track position AND velocity, so the filter can extrapolate where the bus is going, not just where it was. PREDICT applies the constant-velocity motion model F (x += v·Δt) and INFLATES the covariance by adding process noise Q — no new evidence, so uncertainty grows. UPDATE is the clever bit: the Kalman gain K is the precision-weighted blend ratio. Read the gain formula in words — it's "how much of the measurement residual (z − H x̂⁻) do I believe?" When measurement noise R is small (confident GPS), K is large and the estimate snaps to the measurement; when R is large (noisy GPS), K shrinks toward 0 and the estimate leans on the model prediction. The figure makes it concrete in 1-D: the posterior (teal) is a Gaussian that sits BETWEEN the prior and the measurement and is TIGHTER than either — fusing two noisy estimates always reduces variance. H is the observation model (here it just picks out position from the state). filterpy exposes F/H/Q/R explicitly, which is exactly why the demo uses it. Anticipated question: "do I tune K?" — no, K is COMPUTED every step from Q and R, the two knobs you actually choose. Next slide is the same loop drawn as intuition.

The intuition slide (purely "how it works"), sitting between the mechanism slide and the honest-failure beat — spend a minute here, this is the part students find opaque. Walk the LEFT cycle: PREDICT pushes the state forward using the motion model alone, and with no new evidence the uncertainty ellipse GROWS; CORRECT folds in the fresh measurement and the ellipse SHRINKS — the filter forever alternates predict/correct as each new ping arrives. The single dial is the KALMAN GAIN (right panel): the fused estimate lands BETWEEN the model's prediction and the noisy measurement, nearer whichever you trust more. High measurement noise (R big) → low gain → lean on the model; confident measurements → high gain → snap to the data. Contrast with a batch smoother: Kalman is ONLINE — it never looks ahead, it just keeps the best estimate so far, which is why it's the natural real-time tracker (and recurs in U3). Anticipated question: "is the gain something I set?" — no, it's COMPUTED each step from Q and R, the two knobs you actually choose. (If time: this is the same predict/correct skeleton behind particle filters.) Keep the existing two Kalman slides; this one is the bridge that makes the next slide's critique land — a filter this elegant still has no road in it.

The honest-failure beat (demo Step 3). Same figure, now read as a critique: because the filter knows only "smooth motion," it happily draws a graceful curve that slices the corner of the L-bend and sits in a building. It cleaned the wrong problem. This is NOT a dismissal of Kalman — it's the right tool when fixes are dense and jitter dominates (and it returns in U3). It's the wrong tool HERE because the dominant error is the unsampled between-ping route, which no free-space smoother can resolve. The demo's Step 3 shows exactly this: the point-to-road distance shrinks a little but does not collapse. That failure MOTIVATES the HMM, which puts the road network into the model as the transition prior. Set this up as the cliffhanger into Beat 4.

The conceptual scaffold BEFORE casting matching as an HMM — students need the vocabulary "hidden", "emission", "transition" defined generically first. Top of the figure: a plain Markov chain — a few states with arrows carrying transition probabilities a_ij, and the Markov property (memoryless: the future depends only on the present, not the whole past). Bottom: the SAME states, now drawn dashed/greyed to signal they're HIDDEN — you can't see which state you're in. Instead each hidden state emits an OBSERVATION o_t (the teal squares) with some emission probability b_j(o). The inference problem of an HMM: given the observations, recover the most likely hidden-state sequence. That's exactly map matching — next slide names the correspondence (hidden state = road edge, observation = GPS ping). Anticipated question: "why hidden?" — because we never observe which road the bus is on; we only observe noisy GPS, and must infer the road. Keep it generic here; the map-matching mapping is the next slide.

The demo's headline mechanism — spend time here. Walk the figure left to right: each ping (top, grey) is an OBSERVATION; under it sit a few CANDIDATE road edges (hidden states). Two forces score the lattice: the EMISSION links a ping to a candidate (how well does the road explain where the ping landed), and the TRANSITION links consecutive candidates (is moving from this edge to that one a cheap on-road move). Viterbi then finds the single cheapest consistent path through the whole lattice (teal). The key reframe vs Kalman: the matcher scores ROADS, never free space — every ping is explained by an actual edge. The demo Step 4 builds exactly this on the OSMnx graph; an optional skippable cell runs the emission×transition×Viterbi by hand on 3 toy candidates × 2 pings. Define the next two slides' formulas after this picture.

Define the emission model on its own slide (the depth lives here). Read the formula in words off the figure: if the bus is really on this edge, how surprising is a ping that landed d metres away? LEFT panel: the perpendicular distance d from ping to the candidate road edge. RIGHT panel: model the likelihood as a Gaussian in d — small d (ping near the road) is likely; large d is exponentially penalised. The single knob is sigma_z, the assumed GPS noise std, and the figure shows what it controls: a TIGHT sigma (teal) gives a peaky curve that punishes any offset hard, a WIDE sigma (amber) flattens it so even far pings stay plausible. Newson-Krumm fit ~4 m on dashboard GPS; our bus archive is noisier and sparser, so the right sigma is larger and worth SWEEPING (next-but-one slide). The whole "noise is a modeling decision" thesis is literally this number: choose sigma and you've chosen how much to trust each ping. Don't rush — students must feel sigma is a choice they own, not a constant from the library.

The transition model is what Kalman lacked: the ROAD NETWORK as a prior on motion. The figure: LEFT, two consecutive pings with the straight-line gap d_straight drawn beneath, and two candidate on-road routes — a plausible one (teal) that hugs the straight gap and an implausible detour (red dashed) that loops far out of the way. RIGHT, the exponential penalty curve: a small |d_route − d_straight| stays cheap (teal dot), a big mismatch collapses the transition probability (red dot). Read the formula: a transition is plausible when the actual driving distance between the two candidate edges is close to the crow-flies distance between the pings; big mismatches (the route would have to loop or backtrack) are penalised. beta (~0.0959 km in the paper) sets how harshly. This is the term that makes the matcher prefer paths that a vehicle could realistically drive. HONEST CAVEAT for our regime: Newson-Krumm's own sampling-rate experiment shows the plain HMM weakens as the gap grows toward minutes — which is why Lou et al.'s ST-Matching adds a speed/time consistency term for low-sampling-rate GPS. We keep the plain HMM for interpretability and NAME ST-Matching as the upgrade; we don't implement it. Many runs of the same line also average out individual errors (the pooled reveal).

(If short on time, summarize verbally — the figure + the one-line recurrence carry it.) This is HOW the matcher actually computes the answer — students run matcher.match() and get a route, but the engine is Viterbi and it's worth seeing. The figure is a trellis: columns are the pings (time steps), rows are the candidate road edges at each ping; every node holds δ, the best accumulated score of any path that ends in that state. Read the recurrence in words: the best path to state j at time t = the best over all predecessors i of (best-to-i) × (transition a_ij) × (this state's emission b_j(o_t)). So each node only needs the previous column — that's the DP that makes it efficient. You store a BACKPOINTER at each node (which i won), then once you reach the last ping you TRACE BACK right-to-left to recover the actual edge sequence (the teal path + the red backtrace arrow). Complexity O(T·S²): linear in the number of pings T, quadratic in candidates-per-ping S — cheap because we prune to a handful of candidates per ping. Tie-in: the demo's optional skippable cell runs exactly this emission×transition×Viterbi BY HAND on 3 toy candidates × 2 pings, so students can watch the trellis fill. Anticipated question: "is this the same as Dijkstra?" — same DP spirit (optimal substructure), but over a probabilistic lattice, maximising a product of probabilities rather than summing edge weights.

THE clearest "noise is a modeling decision" demonstration, and a direct preview of demo Step 7. The figure: one ping between two parallel candidate roads, with the Gaussian halo growing with sigma. At sigma=5 the prior is tight — the matcher trusts the ping's exact position and commits to the nearer road (and can commit to the WRONG one if the ping is noisy); at sigma=50 the prior is so loose that both roads are plausible and the route can smear or wander. The demo sweeps 5/15/50 m and shows, on a paired line plot, mean residual and matched length moving with sigma, plus per-sigma matched-route layers you can toggle. The lesson to land: there is no single "correct" sigma — it's a deliberate trade-off you tune to your data, and reporting it is part of an honest result. This is the demo cell students will mimic in their own prompts.

Direction is load-bearing and the demo added it deliberately (Step 8, the bearing+velocity-aware matcher; Step 1/5 maps carry direction arrows + START/END markers). The teaching point: a trajectory is not a bag of points — it's ordered and directed, and that ordering carries information the plain distance-only emission throws away. The figure shows the failure mode: a ping sits slightly nearer the westbound carriageway, so distance-only matching snaps it there — but the bus's reported bearing is 96° (eastbound), so it's clearly on the OTHER carriageway. The fix is two interpretable terms, NO torch: a heading-agreement reward on the emission and a velocity gate on the transition. In the demo this flips ~92 nodes on divided Jabotinsky. Connect to DIRECT: students should learn to ASK for direction-aware matching when carriageways matter.

The practice's headline mechanism — walk the four panels. (1) Pile up all the pings and estimate how DENSE they are everywhere; roads light up as ridges. (2) Threshold that density to a binary mask and SKELETONIZE it — thin the ridges to one-pixel-wide centrelines. (3) Find the junctions: skeleton pixels with 3+ neighbours are branch points (intersections); 1-neighbour pixels are endpoints. (4) Trace the chains between junctions into a networkx graph — nodes = junctions, edges = the segments. Cao & Krumm is the seminal "GPS traces -> routable map"; Biagioni & Eriksson is the survey that organises the families and standardises the precision/recall evaluation we use next. There's NO canonical end-to-end library — students assemble scipy + skimage + networkx, which is the point: they DIRECT a multi-step pipeline. The BANDWIDTH (how much you blur) is the inverse-problem twin of the demo's emission sigma — flag that now, it returns two slides on.

Open the black box of the demo/practice's `skimage.skeletonize(method="lee")` — students run it but rarely know what it DOES. Read the three panels: (1) the thresholded KDE is a fat ridge, several pixels across; (2) one THINNING iteration strips the outermost ring of pixels — crucially it removes a pixel only if doing so doesn't break the shape into pieces (connectivity-preserving), so the topology survives; (3) repeat until nothing more can be peeled without disconnecting it — what's left is the 1-pixel medial axis, the centreline. Frame it as the inverse of matching: matching asks "given the road, where's the trajectory"; skeletonization asks "given the ink, where's the road's spine." The quality of the skeleton is set UPSTREAM — too small a bandwidth and the blob is lumpy/disconnected (spurious spurs), too large and parallel roads merge into one ridge — which is exactly why the practice sweeps bandwidth. Anticipated question: "is the skeleton already a graph?" — no, it's still a raster of pixels; turning it INTO nodes and edges is the next slide. (If time: medial axis vs thinning are subtly different; skimage's "lee" is a thinning variant — don't dwell.)

(If short on time, summarize verbally — "peel boundary pixels that don't disconnect the shape, repeat until stable" is the whole idea; the pseudocode is for the curious.) This makes the previous slide's "preserve connectivity" concrete with the actual classic algorithm. Walk the pseudocode: each pass scans every foreground pixel and asks two cheap LOCAL questions about its 8-neighbour ring — (B) how many neighbours are set, and (A) how many times the ring goes 0→1 as you walk around it. The condition 2 ≤ B ≤ 6 means "this is a boundary pixel, not an interior pixel (8) and not an isolated tip (≤1)"; A == 1 is the key connectivity test — exactly one 0→1 transition means the pixel's neighbours form a SINGLE connected arc, so deleting it won't split the shape or punch a hole. The corner conditions (the P2·P4·P6 etc. products in the real algorithm) alternate between two sub-passes so the line stays centred and doesn't erode from one side. The figure shows it converging: the fat T-blob, after two iterations, down to the 1-pixel medial line, with greyed pixels = already deleted. skimage hides all this behind one call; the point of the slide is that it's a SIMPLE, inspectable, deterministic rule — not magic — so students can reason about why a bad bandwidth gives spurs (the blob had bumps the thinning faithfully traced). Anticipated question: "Zhang–Suen vs Lee?" — same family; Lee handles 3-D and is skimage's method="lee"; don't dwell. Next slide turns the 1-px skeleton into nodes and edges.

The last step of the inference pipeline, made mechanical — this is the cell students most often treat as magic. Read the figure: every skeleton pixel is classified by HOW MANY of its 8 neighbours are also skeleton (left). A pixel with ONE neighbour is a dead end → an ENDPOINT node (red); a pixel with TWO is just the middle of a line → NOT a node, it's interior; a pixel with THREE OR MORE is where lines meet → a JUNCTION node (the intersection). Once you've marked the nodes, you walk the deg-2 pixel chains connecting them and each chain becomes one EDGE (right graph). That's the whole assembly: classify pixels → nodes are the non-deg-2 ones → chains between them are edges. `sknw` does this in one call; the demo/practice ship a pure-networkx fallback so it runs even without sknw. Anticipated question: "why is the junction a little cluster of pixels, not one?" — real skeletons fatten slightly at crossings, so a post-step merges adjacent junction pixels into a single node (mention, don't belabor). This is the step whose imperfections (extra spurs, merged junctions) show up as the precision/recall numbers next.

Define the evaluation crisply — students will quote these numbers. PRECISION asks, of every edge you inferred, what fraction lies within tolerance of some real OSM road — it punishes FALSE POSITIVES (the red dashed spur in the figure: a road you drew that isn't there). RECALL asks, of every real OSM road, what fraction has some inferred edge near it — it punishes FALSE NEGATIVES (the grey-only stub: a real road you missed). F1 balances them. Ahmed et al. is the benchmark paper; the research-grade metric is TOPO-F1, but for class we use the buffered/raster simplification: rasterize both networks, dilate by a tolerance (~20 m), and count overlapping pixels — and crucially do it on a RASTER, because shapely buffer-and-union over a whole city is minutes per grade and kills the bandwidth sweep. The next slide is the deep lesson hiding in recall.

The single most important interpretive lesson of the practice — plant it firmly so students don't read a low recall as a broken method. The figure: grey = the full OSM street grid; teal = the subset buses actually drove. Even a PERFECT inference can only recover the teal subset, so recall is structurally capped by COVERAGE, not by your algorithm. That's why the practice's measured grade is precision ≈0.99 (almost everything you drew is a real road — buses really do drive on roads) but recall ≈0.38 (buses only cover ~40% of the grid). The right INTERPRET move is "my recall is coverage-limited, here's the evidence," not "my method failed." This also sets up practice extension (a): per-edge confidence by distinct-vehicle count — and the trap of NOT clipping OSM to the bus corridor before computing recall (which collapses it for a reason that has nothing to do with your method).

The DTW BACKGROUND slide — the mechanism, before the next slide cashes it out for clustering. Read the figure: the two series sit on the axes (B on top, A on the left); the grid is the ACCUMULATED cost D, dark = expensive; the red line is the optimal WARPING PATH, the cheapest monotonic route from bottom-left (align the starts) to top-right (align the ends). The recurrence is the whole algorithm: each cell's accumulated cost = its local mismatch d(i,j) PLUS the cheapest of the three predecessors (down, left, diagonal). The three moves are exactly what lets time warp: a horizontal step = "stay on A, advance B" (B is faster here), a vertical step = the reverse, a diagonal = "advance both in lockstep." Monotonic + continuous means you can't go backwards or skip — order is preserved. Cost O(nm) for length-n and length-m sequences (named on the title). Intuition to land: DTW finds the BEST WAY to stretch/squeeze one time axis onto the other so the SHAPES line up — which is precisely why two runs of the same bus line at different speeds come out similar. (If time: Sakoe-Chiba band caps the warp width and drops cost; tslearn exposes it.) The NEXT slide is the payoff — use this similarity to cluster runs.

Brief but precise — this is the syllabus's original "Euclidean vs DTW" question, preserved as practice extension (c2). The figure: two runs of the same route at different speeds. LEFT, Euclidean matches sample i to sample i (vertical lines) — a bus that dwelled at a stop then sped up is "out of phase," so Euclidean calls two identical-shaped routes DIFFERENT. RIGHT, DTW lets the time axis stretch and compress (diagonal matches) to align by SHAPE, so same-route/different-speed runs come out similar — exactly what you want for variable-speed buses. Name Frechet (worst single-point gap) and LCSS (noise-robust) so all the taught metrics have a definition, even though the demo/practice compute DTW vs Euclidean. tslearn does DTW + DTW-clustering in one dependency. The clustering use: group runs into common-corridor families.

First of two frontier slides — make it feel ALIVE without overclaiming, idea-level only (SLIDE-ONLY, no live code: DiffMM needs a training pass, no shipped checkpoint, so it's out of the demo).
